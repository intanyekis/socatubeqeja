Background

Adam Silver is the fifth and current commissioner of the National Basketball Association (NBA). He was born on April 25, 1962 in Rye, New York, a New York City suburb. He graduated from Rye High School in 1980 and then went on to Duke University, where he majored in political science.

Career

He began working for Les AuCoin, a member of the U.S. House of Representatives as a legislative aid for one year. He then went on to the University of Chicago Law School, where he earned a J.D. in 1988 After law school, Silver worked as a clerk for U.S. District Judge Kimba Wood. Southern District of New York He then worked as an associate at Cravath, Swaine & Moore.

For eight years, Silver was the NBA’s Deputy Commissioner and Chief Operating Officer. He was involved in ventures such as the negotiation of the league’s last three collective bargaining agreements with the National Basketball Players Association, the development of the WNBA league, the development of the NBA Development League, the creation of NBA China, and the partnership with Turner Broadcasting to manage the NBA’s digital assets while in that position.

He has also served as the NBA’s Senior Vice President and Chief Operating Officer, Chief of Staff, and Special Assistant to the Commissioner. Silver worked as an executive producer for NBA Entertainment on the IMAX film “Michael Jordan to the Max,” the documentary “Whatever Happened to Michael Ray?” (2000), and the films “Like Mike” (2002) and “Year of the Yao.”


When David Stern, the NBA Commissioner at the time, announced his intention to resign on February 1, 2014, he endorsed Silver for the position of NBA Commissioner.

Other notable incidents Silver has dealt with as commissioner include Houston Rockets general manager Daryl Morey’s 2019 tweet supporting the 2019 Hong Kong protests.

The tweet provoked political debate as well as questions about China’s relationship with the NBA. China responded negatively to Morey’s tweet, stating that they may decide to cut ties with the NBA. Silver, on the other hand, defended Morey, claiming that he had the right to express himself and free speech.

Silver had received numerous awards and accolades in the sports industry. He was named No. 1 on Sports Business Journal’s 2016 list of the 50 Most Influential People in Sports Business. In 2015, he was named Sports Business Journal’s Executive of the Year, as well as one of Time’s 100 Most Influential People and one of Fortune’s 50 Greatest Leaders. In 2014, Sports Illustrated named him Executive of the Year.
